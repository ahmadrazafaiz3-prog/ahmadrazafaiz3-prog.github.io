Not every lesson from this semester came from a lecture slide. Some of the most important ones came from simply surviving the weeks I least prepared for, and the final exam week of this semester taught me more about discipline than any single course did about programming.

I will be honest about how this happened, because the whole point of documenting my semester here is to be truthful about the experience rather than presenting a polished version of it. The last week before exams was, without exaggeration, one of the most exhausting stretches of my academic life so far. Assignments from multiple courses all seemed to land in the same few days, and on top of that we had quizzes piling up in Digital Logic Design, Database Systems, and Object-Oriented Programming, all within what felt like a single overwhelming window of time. Mornings started early and evenings stretched late, and there were more than a few days where I genuinely struggled to find time to eat a proper meal between classes, labs, and last-minute revision sessions.

Looking back, the root cause was simple and entirely my own responsibility: I did not prepare early enough. Throughout the semester, I had a habit of telling myself I would revise topics "later," convinced that exam week would naturally arrive with enough buffer time to catch up. It did not. Instead, exam week arrived with a backlog of material I should have reviewed weeks earlier, and instead of calmly preparing, I found myself cramming multiple courses worth of content into a handful of sleepless nights.

What made it worse was that this backlog was not evenly distributed. Some weeks during the semester were genuinely lighter, with very little due and very few pressing deadlines, and I distinctly remember choosing to relax during those weeks instead of using that time productively. Dr. Bilal Ahmad, during one of the less hectic weeks earlier in the semester, had actually warned us in class that the workload across our courses tends to compress dramatically near the end, and that spreading out revision early was the only real defense against it. At the time, the warning felt like a generic piece of advice every professor gives. By exam week, I understood exactly how specific and accurate that warning had actually been.

The busiest single day of that week involved a Database Systems quiz in the morning, a Digital Logic Design assignment due by early afternoon, and an Object-Oriented Programming quiz the same evening. I remember sitting in the cafeteria for what felt like five minutes, eating as fast as possible, while mentally running through SQL syntax for the quiz I had right after. It was not a sustainable way to operate, and by the end of that day I felt the kind of exhaustion that comes less from physical effort and more from the constant mental switching between completely different subjects.

There was, admittedly, a useful side effect of all that pressure. Cramming under stress forced me to identify exactly which concepts I actually understood versus which ones I had only pretended to understand throughout the semester. Topics I had genuinely practiced, particularly the Python concepts from earlier in this series, came back to me relatively quickly even under pressure. Topics I had only half-learned took far longer to recall and clearly needed more attention than I had given them during the semester.

By the time exam week ended, I had survived it, but "surviving" is not the same as "doing well by design." The grades I received reflected effort under pressure rather than mastery built over time, and that distinction matters to me. I do not want to repeat this pattern next semester.

The lesson I am taking forward, and writing down here mostly to hold myself accountable, is straightforward: consistent, smaller efforts spread across the semester are far more sustainable and effective than concentrated panic at the end. I plan to treat the lighter weeks of next semester as preparation time rather than rest time, at least when it comes to reviewing material rather than completing new assignments.
